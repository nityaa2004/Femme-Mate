Food to eat during your periods to stay healthy and exercises to do:
During menstruation, it's essential for anyone, including girls, to maintain a balanced diet rich in iron, calcium, and vitamins to support overall health and manage menstrual symptoms. Some foods that may be beneficial during periods include:
Iron-rich foods like spinach, red meat, lentils, and fortified cereals to help replenish iron lost during menstruation.
Foods high in calcium such as dairy products, leafy greens, and fortified non-dairy milk to support bone health and alleviate cramps.
Magnesium-rich foods like nuts, seeds, whole grains, and dark chocolate, which may help reduce bloating and muscle cramps.
Foods high in vitamin B6, such as bananas, chicken, potatoes, and fish, which can help regulate mood swings and reduce water retention.
Omega-3 fatty acids found in fatty fish, flaxseeds, chia seeds, and walnuts, which may help reduce inflammation and ease menstrual pain.
It's also important to stay hydrated by drinking plenty of water and herbal teas to help alleviate bloating and flush out toxins. Additionally, listening to your body and consuming foods that you find comforting and easy to digest can also be beneficial during menstruation.

Medications to have during periods:

Cobra, Cat, Cow and Fish yoga poses are a few good exercises for you to try. They may help you cope with heavy cramping, and are best for your heavier menstrual days. Stretches are best done when the body is warm (as it is after a bath or shower). Each stretch should be held for 30 to 60 seconds.
Because of the slump in energy during menstruation, low-intensity aerobic exercises, such as swimming, yoga, strength training and light cardio – including walking and cycling – can be easier on the body. They're non-weight bearing activities and not very endurance heavy.

SELF-TREATMENT
Taking an anti-inflammatory medication such as ibuprofen or mefenamic acid may help to reduce blood loss.

SEEKING MEDICAL CARE
See a doctor immediately if you:
Soak through more than one pad or tampon an hour over several hours
Also have fatigue or shortness of breath
Make an appointment to see a doctor if you:
Bleed longer than a week or between periods
Notice your menstrual flow is heavier than usual
Pass blood clots on more than one day of your period
Must double-up on pads to prevent soaking through
Have difficulty doing daily activities
Experience any bleeding after menopause
